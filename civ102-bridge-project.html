CIV102

Bridge Project

CIV102 introduced us to the engineering design of structures using theoretical equations and knowledge of how materials react under varying loads. The course culminates in a project where students must design a bridge to support a moving train of increasing weight. The goal is to create a bridge using only the given materials, that sustains as much weight as possible without the bridge breaking apart.

Go to PRAXIS II Check References

Overview

Designed and constructed a matboard and contact cement bridge to maximize load capacity under strict material and geometric constraints. After an iterative designing process, we presented our conclusions through design reports and drawings and tested the bridge.

Stakeholders

Team 106 My team, bridge designers
TAs Evaluators & testers
Classmates Competing Designers
Course Professor Evaluator and course instructor

Bridge Requirements

  • Hold at least a 400N three train car travelling across a 1200mm gap (Load Case 1)
  • Hold at least a 452N three train car, which increased its weight on every pass, travelling across a 1200mm gap (Load Case 2)
  • Made from a matboard with dimensions 32” × 40” × 0.05” and two 30mL tubes of Contact Cement
  • Must be between 1250mm and 1270mm and have a height less than 200mm

Evaluation Criteria

A bridge design with the lowest mass and the highest load capacity would be the best solution.

Design & Construction

Analytical Approach

Hand Calculations

Performed initial calculations for Design 0, covering tension, compression, shear, and glue capacity.

Force Diagrams

Created shear force and bending moment diagrams to map out internal stress across the 1200mm span.

Python Simulation

Used NumPy and Matplotlib to simulate various loads and iteratively optimize design parameters.

Construction Process

Step 1: Matboard Preparation

Cutting of individual matboard pieces to prepare for assembly.

Step 2: Reinforcement & Splices

Installing diaphragms to mitigate shear buckling and gluing the splice sections with contact cement.

Step 3: Final Assembly & Cure

Mounting the top flange and allowing the completed bridge to cure for a minimum of 48 hours before testing.

Final Design

Final Design Features

    1) Double-layer top flange to reduce flexural compression.

    2) Increased web height to improve bending stiffness.

    3) 12 diaphragms to mitigate shear buckling.

    4) Splice offset from the middle of the bridge.

Results & Reflection

Results

Predicted failure load: ~870 N
Dominant predicted failure mode: flexural compression
Actual outcome: premature failure at 0 N due to poor splice construction.

Due to an oversight while making our splice construction, our bridge failed on the first load case of an empty train. Despite using all our knowledge to create a strong design, we failed to build the right splice connection, compromising our other efforts.

Reflection

My most notable contributions in the design part of this project were doing hand calculations for design 0, produced engineering drawings showing critical features of the bridge, contributing to the iteration strategy. I also played a major role in bridge construction and assembly, allowing me to participate in both design and engineering. Finally, I helped compile requirements and design choices in a concise but informative design report, summarizing and justifying our efforts

In this case, theoretical performance did not equate to real-world success, as our idealized models did not fully capture the vulnerabilities of our physical designs. In this project, the actual outcome was heavily influenced by construction quality—particularly the design and reliability of connections. My team had not put as much thought and effort into designing the splice, as to designing the rest of the bridge. It was a misunderstanding on our part of what the weakest points of our bridge were and where we should have focused our efforts. However, through lived experiences like this, I was able to learn about what should be prioritized in engineering design. This experiential knowledge only comes through doing real-word design projects but makes a large impact in the grand scheme of engineering design, which is why I chose to add it as my fourth pillar.

CTMFs Used in This Project

CTMF 1 — Requirements

FRAMING

What it is

Requirements are a framing tool that allow the designers to define their design space. They often come from the objectives outlined in the opportunity or from specific stakeholder expectations. Requirements can lead to the creation of evaluation criteria, which help in determining which design best meets the objectives. Overall, requirements help guide the thought process when approaching a design challenge.

How Did it Help

“Build a bridge” is an extremely vague demand. There are many kinds of bridges that each serve different functions. At the beginning of the bridge project, defining requirements played a critical role in shaping our approach to this project. These requirements outlined the minimum criteria our bridge needed to satisfy, such as load capacity, material constraints, and dimensional limits. By using these expectations to guide our design, we were able to better understand the problem and ensure that our design decisions aligned with the project goals. Furthermore, the requirements helped us understand what the key things were that this project was looking for. Once we were able to meet the minimum requirements, we used them to figure out which criteria we wanted our bridge design to excel in.

What Did I learn

Working with requirements showed me the importance of properly framing a problem before attempting to solve it. I learned that clearly defined criteria not only provide direction, but also help ensure that the final design is both feasible and aligned with expectations. In the future, I will prioritize establishing strong requirements at the start of any project to guide my thinking and decision-making.

CTMF 2 — Iterating on designs

DIVERGING

What it is

When it came to developing our solution, we used iterating on designs as a diverging tool. Iterating involves repeatedly modifying and refining a design to explore multiple possible solutions. Iteration works by cycling through repeated versions of a design, where each version introduces changes based on feedback, testing, or new ideas. Iterating on designs helps expand the design space by introducing new ideas.

How Did it Help

Iterating on designs allowed us to continuously improve our cross-section design to perform better against the evaluation criteria. Each prototype gave us an additional option to choose from and test and also helped us better understand what influenced the strength of our bridge design. After doing calculations for each iteration, we were able to use the results we got to guide improvements. For example, recognizing that increasing the glue tab width would increase the strength of our design was an iteration that made it to our final design. Over time, this helped us identify crucial design features that formed the fundamental parts of our final bridge design.

What Did I learn

Using iteration taught me that strong designs rarely emerge fully formed—they develop over time through repeated refinement. I learned that progress often comes from exploring multiple versions rather than perfecting a single idea early. Moving forward, I will use iteration more deliberately to ensure I explore a wider range of possibilities before converging on a final design.

CTMF 3 — Proxy Testing

REPRESENTING

What it is

We used proxy testing as a representing tool to test our design and learn how it would react under the predicted load cases. Proxy testing works by using indirect representations such as simulations, calculations, or code to approximate how a system would behave in real life when physical prototyping is not possible. It is extremely useful as it allows the designers to represent and demonstrate the intended function of the design when it is not feasible to create a fully working prototype.

How Did it Help

Proxy testing allowed us to evaluate performance despite constraints in time, materials, and fabrication. By using code-based simulations, we were able to test bridge behaviour, make iterations to our design, and compare results without building a physical bridge prototype. This made it easier to explore multiple scenarios quickly and identify important features during the design process. Furthermore, with code there was no concern about building multiple prototypes. Performing proxy testing with code allowed extensive repetition and testing.

What Did I learn

Using proxy testing showed me how using the right representing tools for a design can be just as valuable as physical testing in engineering design. I learned that simulations and code can effectively model system behaviour and support decision-making when real-world testing is limited. Moving forward, I will use proxy testing earlier in the design process to validate ideas before committing to fabrication. Moreover, proxy testing can help build an argument when trying to recommend a design because it proves that certain features work in practical applications.
