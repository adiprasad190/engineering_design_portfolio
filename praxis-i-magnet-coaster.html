PRAXIS I Project

Magnet Coaster

Our design project in PRAXIS I focused on addressing a “splartz” we had discovered at the beginning of the term. A “splartz” was a word created for the course to describe a small yet recurring issue in our daily lives. The final goal of the project was to create a design report, recommending a solution to our “splartz” of choice.

Stakeholders

PRAXIS I Group Group 34, The Designers
Primary Users UofT students, staff, and professors
Secondary Users UofT custodial staff
Other Stakeholders Regulatory bodies (e.g., Canadian government)

The Splartz

Cups, coffee mugs, and water bottles used by University of Toronto staff and students leave ringed stains on surfaces across campus, ruining the aesthetic of furniture such as desks and tables.

The Goals

  • A design that prevents stains from forming
  • A design that is compatible with multiple sizes of drinking vessels
  • A design that is ergonomic and does not hinder the user
  • A design that has a long lifespan
  • A design that is safe for the user to use

Design Process

Alpha Release

A midway point in the course, towards the end of our diverging process, that presented our understanding of the design brief and early concept ideas, generated from diverging.

Potential Solutions

After many iterations and using diverging tools, we arrived at 3 possible designs plus the reference design.

  • Magnetic Coaster
  • Liquid Collecting Sleeve
  • Absorptive Clamp
  • Coaster (Reference Design)

Testing and Evaluation

  • Conducted testing of all 3 potential solutions and the reference design
  • Created a measurement matrix with the data collected during testing
  • Compared potential solutions to one another using Pugh charts to complete converging process

Results & Reflection

Results

The magnet coaster prototype demonstrated the strongest overall performance when evaluated against our evaluation criteria. It consistently outperformed the other proposed solutions in key areas such as liquid impediment, intervessel compatibility and convenience to the user.

Our design report presented all of our work, including the problem framing, concept development, evaluation process, and final justification, into a clear and structured document. This allows a third party to not only understand our final recommendation but also follow the reasoning and evidence that led us to select the magnet coaster as the most effective solution.

Reflection

My notable contributions to this design project were in designing a solution, building a physical prototype, and writing the design report. These activities taught me the engineering design process, showing the structure, thought, and representation that goes behind creating any design.

However, perhaps more importantly, PRAXIS I helped redefine the way I approached teamwork in a design situation. My experiences changed my view of collaboration by enlightening me with new strategies which impacted the way I worked. To dive more in depth to how these changed my thinking, let’s dive into some of the CTMFs I used throughout this project.

Team Contributions

  • Potential designs and brainstorming were group efforts.
  • The Design Brief document was shared team work.
  • Building of prototypes and testing were group efforts.

CTMFs Used in This Project

CTMF 1 — Brainstorming 6-3-5

DIVERGING

What it is

When it came to generating ideas for Alpha Release, my team thought it would be difficult to generate n + 1 different solutions, so the task of generating 50 ideas seemed impossible. However, the key to overcoming our problem was using diverging tools, specifically “Brainwriting 6-3-5”. Brainwriting 6-3-5 works by having each group member simultaneously draw three possible solutions and then pass it off to the member to the right. The receiving member can now analyze the other person’s ideas and modify it so that it better meets requirements. We did one full loop so that everyone saw each person’s ideas at least once. In the end, we managed to have a chart paper full of many viable solutions to our opportunity.

How Did it Help

As aforementioned, Brainwriting 6-3-5 is a diverging tool and relates to the diverging strand in the Praxis FDCR model. At first, it sounded counterintuitive to me that diverging in a group project would help generate ideas. With Brainwriting 6-3-5, I was able to independently put down whatever ideas I had without my thought process being interrupted by someone else’s suggestion. Then when I received their ideas, I was able to analyze their thinking and build upon it from a completely new perspective. Thus, I was indirectly working together with my groupmates by building off their ideas and sharing my ideas with them. This helped create a huge pool of designs that we all somewhat agreed on and could move forward with. Another thing that I enjoyed about Brainwriting 6-3-5 was that I did not have to worry about any of my ideas being dismissed quickly by other team members. When using the previous conversational format, any ideas that were not immediately agreed on by the team were rejected and ignored. However, with Brainwriting 6-3-5 I was able to write down whatever ideas I had and my team members were forced to consider how they could be a viable solution. This enabled a lot more solutions to be brought into the pool of ideas and with a physical drawing, it could not be easily forgotten either.

What Did I learn

Using diverging has completely changed my perspective of teamwork and made me more accepting of this idea that it is possible to work separately yet still collaborate. Moving forward, I see myself using more of diverging tools like Brainstorming 6-3-5, SCAMPER, or Lotus Blossom when entering group environments because I think they enable me to use my own creative freedom without being impeded upon by others. Additionally, it keeps core principles of teamwork, such as listening to other perspectives and building off each other’s ideas. Moving forward, anytime I must generate solutions or ideas to an open problem I would work independently before presenting my ideas to groupmates and integrating their perspectives into my thinking.

CTMF 2 — Pugh Charts

CONVERGING

What it is

When it came to narrowing down our potential solutions, our team needed a structured way to evaluate which concept best met our criteria. To do this, we used a Pugh chart, a converging tool that compares different design options depending on how well they do when tested for specific evaluation criteria. It generally takes one design as the reference and then compares the other designs through adjacent columns, signalling with set characters if the design is better, worse, or the same in the evaluation criteria. This allows groups to systematically identify strengths and weaknesses in certain prototypes and determine which prototype can consistently perform well across all evaluation criteria. Essentially, pugh charts simplify the converging process by summarizing how prototypes perform allowing the design team to make a evidence-backed decision.

How Did it Help

We used Pugh charts after conducting testing and organizing all our data in a measurement matrix. The Pugh chart we found most useful was the one with the magnet coaster as reference. The magnet coaster either performed better or the same in every evaluation criterion other than one, against all the other designs. It demonstrated to our group that the magnet coaster was clearly performing better in relevant tests and must be our strongest design. Since it was an evidence-backed representation of which design was best, it reduced disagreement in the group about which design was best. It created a shared understanding which allowed us to be efficient in our decision-making process and continue forward with other tasks.

What Did I learn

Using a Pugh chart helped rework how I approach proposing a design in a collaborative design environment. Using a Pugh chart helps remove biases since they use evidence-backed comparisons. It helped speed up the process of choosing a design, which in previous group projects we would likely debate over for long periods of time. It also create a shared understanding that no one could dispute which design would perform the best. Moving forward, I plan to use Pugh charts in future collaborative design projects whenever I need to compare multiple potential solutions, since they lead to evidence-backed decision making. Beyond engineering, I can also see myself applying this tool in other important life choices, because of how effectively it summarizes and compares complex data.

CTMF 3 — Prototyping

REPRESENTING

What it is

Prototyping is a representing tool and serves as an easy method to represent our ideas without actually building them. They are often built on smaller scales, using cheaper materials, or without all features involved, instead isolating the most important parts. Prototypes provide a proof of concept, imagining what a design will look like and demonstrating some of its most unbelievable functions. Prototypes also allow for proxy testing, demonstrating why the proposed concept would work and helping the designers to form a more convincing argument. Lastly, by translating concepts into a prototype, the designers can identify areas for improvement and use that to help present their idea with next steps or prepare for counterclaims.

How Did it Help

Our prototype of the magnet coaster captured many of our intentional design choices and decisions we made throughout the process. Furthermore, it was able to represent the concept of attachment using velcro pads. While not the magnet design that is intended for the final solution, it achieved its purpose of letting others understand our design decisions and overall approach with this solution.

What Did I learn

Prototyping showed me the importance of physically representing ideas in the engineering design process. I learned that even simple models can reveal critical insights and help refine a design in ways that theoretical analysis cannot. In the future, I see myself using prototypes as a way to test assumptions, communicate ideas more effectively, and iterate on designs before finalizing them. To me, prototyping is a new form of communication that can be used when working in a team.
