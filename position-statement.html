Position Statement

Engineering Design

to me, is a collaborative process of applying scientific knowledge to create innovative, sustainable solutions that address real-world problems. It is driven not only by technical problem-solving, but by a responsibility to design and create meaningful impact through thoughtful design.

See Project Application Review Sources

Definition Summary

Engineering: Applying science and knowledge to create innovative tools that have the potential to cause change

Design: the process of coming up with ideas that address certain requirements or criteria

Engineering design: Applying science and knowledge to create innovative tools that address certain objectives, often described as creating a solution to an opportunity

“Engineering or technology is all about using the power of science to make life better for people, to reduce cost, to improve comfort, to improve productivity, etc.” — N. R. Narayana Murthy

Entering into university, my understanding of engineering design was based off three core beliefs

Designing and implementing sustainable solutions

How this aligned with my definition?

It emphasizes the application of scientific knowledge to create innovative yet sustainable tools to address real-world problems.

Removing barriers and increasing living standards through innovative design

How this aligned with my definition?

Engineering design can be used to remove barriers and increase living standards through the creation of innovative solutions.

The importance of teamwork and collaboration

How this aligned with my definition?

Engineering design is a collaborative process that requires teamwork and collaboration to be successful. Working with team members allows the accumulation of knowledge that can be used to approach designs.

Designing and Implementing Sustainable Solutions

In grade 10 and 11, I began to dive deeper into the global issue of climate change and the debate of whether it is a hoax or the Earth’s number one enemy. However, underlying all the data and opinions, the bigger root problem that has likely led to climate change is the unsustainability of human societies. Since the industrial revolution, we have consistently sacrificed sustainable practices in favour of convenience. This idea of “if it stills works, don’t fix it”, has led us to deplete non-renewable resources and pay little attention to sustainable, environmentally friendly alternatives. As I continued researching how humans can make themselves more sustainable, I realized that finding new solutions was tied in with engineering design. My two favourite topics became nuclear power generation and hydrogen powered electric vehicles, both excellent examples of how applied science in the process of engineering design can help find alternatives to our current, non-sustainable societal practices.

Removing barriers and increasing living standards through innovative design

My stance on engineering design also stems from my family origins. My parents and grandparents are from the state of Bihar in Northeast India. I have only been to Bihar twice in my life, once when I was 5 years old and again after graduating high school. The second trip helped me recognize the disparity between my life in Canada and my close family members in my home village. I was shocked to find out that things which I considered bare necessities like electricity, internet, and clean temperature-controlled water were privileges or even inaccessible, due to improper system designs. Through iterative and well-structured engineering design processes, these flaws could have been avoided, and it would ensure basic needs are not defined by geographical areas, but rather a commonality for all humans globally.

The importance of teamwork and collaboration

I think collaboration and communication are absolutely imperative to engineering design. When working on any problem, I enjoy working with a team because different perspectives when combined can create stronger solutions. During my time on student council throughout high school, I experienced and witnessed so many moments where unique ideas suggested by different people would combine into something that was seemingly perfect. Also, everybody has different strengths and experiences which invites holistic approaches to problems. When I played competitive soccer, I realized that everyone on the team may have known the basics but were also specialized in certain skills. Not a single person on the team was an expert on everything, so the team only played well if we worked together and understood each other’s capabilities. Thus, I believe a good engineering design process is nothing without strong collaboration and communication.

But after my first year, my understanding of engineering design has evolved significantly. My original pillars have been reshaped and expanded upon because of new experiences.

The Evolved Position: How Did My Understanding Change?

Pillar 1: Designing and Implementing Sustainable Solutions

My perspective for this pillar has not changed much. I still believe engineering design should focus on creating sustainable and innovative tools to change the world. However, I have developed a deeper appreciation for the complexity of the design process. PRAXIS II taught me that stakeholder values, codes and standards, and constraints such as time, cost, and feasibility often shape and limit the design space. This realization has lead me to the understanding that the ideal solution is not always the most practical one

Pillar 2: Removing barriers and increasing living standards through innovative design

I’ve come to realize that my earlier perspective was naïve and too broad. PRAXIS I & II highlighted that different communities face seemingly similar yet unique challenges, and there is rarely a single, one size fits-all solution for them all. Furthermore, I learned that those big ideas that seem to be the perfect solution don’t just appear on the first attempt at a problem. They actually come from starting with small designs and iteratively refining them in accordance to the specific problem.

Pillar 3: The importance of teamwork and collaboration

Still a fundamental part of my view of engineering design but how I approach it now has changed from my PRAXIS I experiences. PRAXIS I helped redefine my understanding of effective teamwork and the introduction of certain CTMFs made me reconsider how I approach collaboration.

Pillar 4??? Learning through lived experiences and failure

During my first year, I decided to add a new pillar to my understanding of engineering design. This new pillar was inspired by the CIV102 project. During the CIV102 Bridge project, I realized that engineering design is not all about theory and what works on paper. There are crucial lessons to be learned from experimental work and lived experiences.

Furthermore, I have began to realize that while my values used for Pillar 1 and 2 are critical to my understanding of engineering design, they are not the best way to define my understanding. Based on the lessons I have learned about them throughout my first year, I have refined my pillars of engineering design to reflect their importance but also provide a more defined understanding.

The Evolved Position: My Four Pillars of Engineering Design

Using applied science and knowledge to create the most practical and sustainable solutions

This combines the original pillar of designing and implementing sustainable solutions with the lessons learned about how the most ideal design is not always the most practical. I am still influenced by my value to chase the best solutions to make our societies more sustianable, but I am now more informed about the multiple factors that are behind engineering design.

Iterating through designs to discover new innovative and impactful solutions

This pillar is a direct reflection of the lessons I learned during PRAXIS II and the CIV102 Bridge project. Small iterations upon designs are the best way to achieving bright, novel ideas. Furthermore, by using iterations, we can adjust our designs to the specifications of certain communities, considering their unique circumstances. This removes the optimistic but impractical one size-fits-all solution.

The importance of teamwork and collaboration

Through my PRAXIS I experiences, I have reworked my understanding of collaborative design and have adopted new strategies that should help me in the future.

Learning through lived experiences and failure

The CIV102 Bridge project was a defining moment in my understanding of engineering design. It taught me the importance of prior knowledge and how it can be used to approach designs. Real-world experience is something to be considered when proceeding in engineering design. It's not all about theory.

The rest of my portfolio will further explore how my understanding of engineering design has evolved through these three projects: PRAXIS I, CIV102 Bridge, and PRAXIS II
