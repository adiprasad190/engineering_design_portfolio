PRAXIS II Project

Rock Climbing Handhold Motor Brush

Our design project in PRAXIS II took the fundamentals we learned in PRAXIS I and brought it to a larger scale. This time we worked with a local community to design a solution to one of their opportunities. The final goal of the project was converge on a solution and present it in an oral presentation known as Showcase.

Overview

Background & Opportunity

Climbers at Basecamp Climbing Gym leave chalk residue on climbing holds, reducing texture and grip. Current cleaning methods either require considerable manual effort, fail to reach tight crevasses, or release large amounts of airborne chalk dust.

Key Design Objectives

  • Maximize Cleaning Effectiveness
  • Minimize dust
  • Maximize Reachability
  • Protect Holds
  • Preserve Climbing Experience
  • Ensure Safety

Results & Reflection

Results

“ASAP, Rocky” excelled in achieving its design objectives and showed great potential as an implementable solution. At Showcase, we were able to present how its combination of a motorized brush and vinegar cleaning reduces the manual effort required from the user and achieves its functional objectives. Despite the limitations the design currently faces, there are already next steps which could be improved upon, highlighting that engineering design is not always a one-shot at creating the big perfect idea, but rather iterations of smaller design changes to get closer and closer to an ideal solution.

Reflection

Although the design uses some core features of the original reference design, such as the painter’s pole, it makes small modifications that help the design perform better in evaluation criteria. This process of improving upon reference designs allows us as designers to implement tried and tested ideas to direct our thinking.

Notable contributions I made to this project began with reframing the opportunity. We had to change our community after receiving the RFP, resulting in an adjustment of requirements to align with the specific opportunity. Despite being a similar general problem, differences in features such as reference designs, stakeholders, and requirement metrics forced us to rethink how we scoped our design space to best align with our community. I also took a major role in prototype designing and manufacturing, which reinforced my understanding of how lived experiences play a major role in engineering design. Some of the major challenges that we faced while designing our prototype was solved through prior knowledge of certain technologies.

CTMFs Used in This Project

CTMF 1 — Stakeholder Analysis

FRAMING

What it is

Stakeholder analysis is a framing tool that looks at stakeholder values, expectations, and interactions and identifies the needs, goals, and constraints of the people affected by the design. It works by systematically examining different user groups (stakeholders) to determine their priorities and requirements, which then shapes how the design problem is framed. It helps in bounding the design space and giving the designers a better understanding of how to approach their opportunity.

How Did it Help

Stakeholder analysis helped us reframe our entire design opportunity when we switched communities. Even though both groups were rock climbing communities, their needs and contexts were different enough that our original requirements were no longer fully valid. We had to redo and adjust our requirements to match the new stakeholder specifications. For example, one major change we had to make was that our new community had a bouldering wall height of 5.5m, rather than former community’s 4m high wall. Additionally, the reference designs at our new community were different requiring us to rescope our design space to understand what is already available. Performing these small adjustments based on the present stakeholders ensured our final design was aligned with the needs of the people we were designing for.

What Did I learn

Using stakeholder analysis taught me that design is highly context dependent. I learned that even within similar domains, different user groups can have very different needs, meaning there is rarely a perfect, one-size-fits-all solution. Moving forward, I will prioritize understanding stakeholders when approaching engineering design and minimizing my assumptions so that I can create a design that is actually applicable.

CTMF 2 — Reference Design

FRAMING

What it is

We used reference designs as a framing tool, to help define and structure the initial problem space by drawing on existing solutions. Reference designs work by examining pre-existing products or systems and using them as a foundation to understand what features are already in use, how constraints can influence designs, and what approaches are there to this opportunity. Although reference designs are often thought as what not to do, they can be useful in the sense that they can be iterated upon so that their negative features can be replaced and their positive features retained.

How Did it Help

Reference designs helped us establish a starting point in the design space by giving us a concrete example with the brush attached to a painter’s pole. Although we had our idea of the motorized brush head combined with vinegar already confirmed, we were unsure about how to achieve the height requirement of reaching 5.5m. Using the painter’s pole found in the reference design, we began to generate ideas and eventually decided it was the best method currently available to us. However, it could still be modified to be better which is when we began by making small iterations on it, to better adapt to the current problem. Eventually, we changed the brush to add a pivoting head that allowed the user to adjust easier to angled surfaces, something missing in the reference design.

What Did I learn

Using reference designs helped me understand that engineering design is fundamentally iterative and evolutionary rather than fully original from the start. I learned that strong ideas often emerge by building on existing solutions and gradually refining them. Moving forward, I will use reference designs more intentionally to quickly enter unfamiliar design spaces and generate meaningful early concepts, while still allowing room for innovation and iteration.

CTMF 3 — CAD Modelling

REPRESENTING

What it is

When it came to representing our final design, we used CAD modelling as a representing tool. CAD (Computer-Aided Design) allows engineers to create precise 3D models of components and assemblies. It can help used in visually communicate ideas, structures, and functional behaviour in a detailed and measurable format. It enables designers to translate abstract concepts, sketches, and design decisions into a structured model that can be shared, analysed, and refined throughout the design process. Furthermore, CAD modelling can be used to model 3D printed parts which are sometimes necessary for prototype development

How Did it Help

In our project CAD acted as a complete visual summary of our design process from ideation to final solution. It allowed us to bring together all the decisions made during brainstorming, iteration, and prototyping into one coherent model. Different components of the CAD assembly reflected different contributions from team discussions, showing how ideas were combined, modified, and improved over time. For example, the ball bearing in the CAD was one of our most critical ideas during brainstorming, as it allows the combination of a stationary and rotary component in our device. This is visible in the CAD, as the ball bearing allows the rotary parts to revolve in the simulation while the stationary parts stay fixed. CAD for us was not just a final product representation, but also evidence of our collaborative process and engineering reasoning.

What Did I learn

Using CAD modelling showed me that representation is a crucial tool in engineering design, especially when developing a proposed solution. I learned that CAD helps connect thinking and building by turning ideas into something measurable and testable. Moving forward, I will use CAD more intentionally as a communication tool to clearly show both the final design and the reasoning behind it.
